# Part-time Work Rules for Students in Korea

## Balancing Study and Work in South Korea
Studying in South Korea is an incredible experience. From Seoul’s bustling streets to the scenic views of Busan, the country offers world-class education. However, living costs in metropolitan areas can add up. Rent, food, and transport often strain a student's budget. Fortunately, the Korean government allows international students to work part-time (known as *Alba* or 아르바이트).

Working part-time helps offset expenses, lets you practice your Korean, and helps you integrate into the local community. However, South Korea enforces strict rules. Working illegally can lead to heavy fines, visa cancellation, or deportation. This KR Campus guide explains the legal rules, visa restrictions, working hour limits, and job search process so you can work safely.

## The Legal Framework: Visa Types and Eligibility
To work legally in South Korea, you must hold a valid student visa and obtain official permission from the immigration office before starting. You cannot start working immediately upon arrival.

*   **D-2 Visa (Degree-Seeking Students):** Enrolled in undergraduate or postgraduate programs, you can apply for part-time work permission once you receive your Alien Registration Card (ARC).
*   **D-4 Visa (Language Students):** If you are studying Korean at a university language institute, you must wait **six months** (180 days) from your entry date before you can apply for a work permit.

Both visa holders must maintain good academic standing. Language students need an attendance rate of 90% or higher. Degree students must keep a GPA of 2.0 or above (out of 4.5) in the previous semester. If your grades or attendance drop, your work permit application will be denied.

## Working Hours and TOPIK-Based Limits
The Ministry of Justice limits the hours international students can work. These limits are tied to your degree level and Korean language proficiency, measured by the Test of Proficiency in Korean (TOPIK).

If you do not meet the minimum TOPIK requirement for your study level, your allowed hours are cut in half, usually restricting you to just 10 hours per week. It pays to study Korean diligently!

| Student Status / Degree Level | TOPIK Requirement | Weekday Hour Limit (With TOPIK) | Weekday Hour Limit (No/Low TOPIK) | Weekend & Vacation Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Language Student (D-4) | TOPIK 2 or higher | 20 hours/week | 10 hours/week | 20 hours (No vacation change) |
| Undergraduate (Y1-Y2) | TOPIK 3 or higher | 20 hours/week | 10 hours/week | Unlimited during vacations |
| Undergraduate (Y3-Y4) | TOPIK 4 or higher | 25 hours/week | 10 hours/week | Unlimited during vacations |
| Postgraduate (Master's/PhD) | TOPIK 4 or higher | 30 hours/week | 15 hours/week | Unlimited during vacations |

*Note:* "Unlimited during vacations" only applies to regular semesters' scheduled breaks (summer and winter) for degree-seeking students (D-2). Language students (D-4) are always restricted to their weekly limit.

## Allowed and Prohibited Jobs for International Students
While there are many opportunities, Korea has strict rules regarding the industries international students can enter.

### Common and Allowed Jobs
*   **Convenience Stores & Retail:** Cashiering and stocking shelves (GS25, CU, 7-Eleven). Great for conversational practice.
*   **Cafes and Restaurants:** Serving, barista work, or kitchen help. The most common jobs for intermediate speakers.
*   **On-Campus Roles:** Library duties, administrative support, or teaching assistantships. These are flexible and convenient.
*   **Tourism & Duty-Free:** Roles utilizing your native language (English, Chinese, or Japanese) in major tourist spots.

### Prohibited Jobs
Students are barred from working in heavy industries, manufacturing, construction, adult entertainment, and private tutoring (unless specifically authorized).

| Allowed Industries | Prohibited Industries |
| :--- | :--- |
| Cafes, Restaurants & Bakeries | Manufacturing & Factory Work |
| Convenience Stores & Retail | Construction Sites & Physical Labor |
| On-Campus Assistantships | Private Tutoring (Without special permit) |
| Tourism & Duty-Free Shops | Adult Entertainment & Bars |
| Office Admin & Translation | Multi-level Marketing Schemes |

## How to Get Your Work Permit: A Step-by-Step Guide
Never work before receiving your permit. Doing so is illegal and carries heavy penalties. Follow this step-by-step process to get authorized:

1.  **Secure a Job Offer:** You must find a job first; the employer must sign your forms.
2.  **Fill out the Confirmation Form:** Download the Part-time Work Confirmation Form (시간제취업 확인서). You and your employer must complete it.
3.  **Get University Approval:** Take the form, the employer's business registration copy, and your academic transcripts to your university’s international office for signing.
4.  **Submit to Immigration:** Submit the application online via Hikorea (hikorea.go.kr) or visit your local immigration office.

**Required Documents Checklist:**
*   Passport and Alien Registration Card (ARC)
*   Signed Part-time Work Confirmation Form
*   Standard Labor Contract (showing wage, hours, and duties)
*   Copy of Employer's Business Registration
*   Academic Transcript and Certificate of Enrollment
*   TOPIK Score Report

Approval takes 5 to 10 business days. A digital confirmation will update your official visa status.

## Practical Tips for Job Hunting in Korea
*   **Minimum Wage:** Always ensure your employment contract pays the legal rate. As of 2024, the minimum wage in South Korea is 9,860 KRW per hour. This rate applies to international students just as it does to local citizens.
*   **Job Search Portals:** Use specialized Korean job search apps like *Albamon* (알바몬) and *Alba Heaven* (알바천국). These platforms are entirely in Korean, so knowing basic reading skills is vital. You can also search your university's bulletin boards for on-campus vacancies.
*   **Taxes and Fees:** A standard 3.3% income tax is typically withheld from your monthly part-time earnings. This is a normal practice under local tax laws, and you do not need to worry about additional tax filing unless you earn a very high income.
*   **Prioritize Academics:** Your student visa is designed for academic pursuit. Keep your attendance high and grades steady. Your work permit will not be renewed if your GPA drops below 2.0 or if your class attendance falls below required minimums.
