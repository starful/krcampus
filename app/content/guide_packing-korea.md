# What to Pack for Korea Study Abroad: The Ultimate Seasonal Checklist

Preparing to study abroad in South Korea is an exciting milestone. Whether you are heading to a prestigious university in Seoul like SNU, Yonsei, or Korea University, or exploring campuses in coastal cities like Busan, packing efficiently is key. South Korea experiences four distinct seasons, meaning your wardrobe needs strategic planning. Beyond clothes, navigating administrative entry requirements, local electronic standards, and health system variations is crucial for a stress-free start.

To help you avoid common pitfalls—like bringing heavy appliances that will fry in Korean outlets or packing bulky home essentials you can buy cheap at Daiso—KR Campus has put together this ultimate guide. Let's look at what to pack before you fly.

## 1. Essential Documents and Financial Preparation
Your most critical assets are your administrative and financial lifelines. You must present physical copies of several documents upon arrival at Incheon Airport and during university registration. Keep these in your carry-on luggage so they do not get lost.

While South Korea is highly digitized, carrying cash (Korean Won) is essential for your first few days before you open a local bank account or receive your Alien Registration Card (ARC), which takes three to four weeks.

| Document Type | Primary Purpose | Backup Needed? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Passport & Visa (D-2/D-4)** | Legal entry and resident stay. | Yes, save PDF on cloud. |
| **Admission Certificate (CoA)** | Immigration and school check-in. | Yes, print 2 backup copies. |
| **Tuberculosis Test Results** | Mandatory for dormitory entry. | Yes, keep original paper copy. |
| **Apostilled Academic Records** | Required for school registration. | Yes, keep in sealed envelope. |
| **International Driving Permit** | Optional, but useful for renting scooters. | Yes, bring physical booklet. |

**Financial Pro-Tip:** Inform your bank of your travel. Bring two credit/debit cards (Visa or Mastercard work best). Keep 250,000 to 300,000 KRW in cash for immediate costs like transit cards and airport meals.

## 2. Electronics, Voltage, and Staying Connected
South Korea is a global tech hub, but its electrical standards might differ from your home country. Understanding these differences protects your devices.

*   **Voltage and Plugs:** South Korea uses **Type C** and **Type F** outlets (220V at 60Hz).
*   **Adapters vs. Converters:** If coming from North America (110V) or the UK (230V), plug adapters work for dual-voltage devices (phones, laptops). High-draw heating elements (hair dryers, straighteners) from 110V countries will burn out; buy these locally.
*   **Power Strips:** Pack one multi-plug power strip from your home country. Plug it into a single Korean adapter to charge multiple devices at once.
*   **SIM Cards:** Ensure your phone is carrier-unlocked. Buy a temporary 30-day tourist SIM at Incheon Airport to stay connected until you secure a cheaper student contract with your ARC.

## 3. Health, Medicine, and Personal Care Essentials
South Korea has excellent healthcare, but finding exact Western formulations of medicines can be challenging.

*   **Prescription Medicine:** Bring a 3-to-6-month supply with an English doctor's note. Certain ADHD medications (like Adderall) are strictly illegal in South Korea. Check with the Korean Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) well in advance for import clearance.
*   **Health Insurance:** International students are enrolled in the Korean National Health Insurance Service (NHIS) after registration, but coverage only kicks in once your ARC is ready. Buy temporary travel insurance for your first month.
*   **OTC Medications:** Bring your favorite brands of painkillers (Ibuprofen/Acetaminophen), cold medicine, and stomach relief. Explaining symptoms in Korean during your first week can be daunting.
*   **Deodorant:** Deodorant is not commonly used by locals in Korea, and selection is highly limited. Pack enough to last your entire stay.
*   **Skincare:** Do not overpack cosmetics. Korea is the capital of K-Beauty; you can easily buy affordable, world-class products anywhere.

## 4. Seasonal Clothing and Weather Guide
Korea experiences extreme seasonal shifts. Winters are bitterly cold (dropping below -10°C / 14°F), while summers are intensely hot, humid, and rainy due to the monsoon season (*Jangma*) in July.

| Season | Months | Average Temp Range | Essential Wardrobe Items |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Spring** | Mar - May | 10°C to 22°C (50°F to 72°F) | Light jackets, cardigans, sneakers. |
| **Summer** | Jun - Aug | 23°C to 35°C (73°F to 95°F) | Breathable linen, t-shirts, light raincoat. |
| **Autumn** | Sep - Nov | 10°C to 20°C (50°F to 68°F) | Denim jackets, sweaters, layers. |
| **Winter** | Dec - Feb | -15°C to 5°C (5°F to 41°F) | Down parka, thermal underwear, boots. |

### Packing Tips by Arrival Season
*   **The "Long Padding" Rule:** If staying for the winter, you will need a knee-length down jacket (called "long padding"). These are incredibly bulky. Buy one in Korea where they are cheap, stylish, and built for local wind-chill.
*   **The Shoe Factor:** Hilly campus walks require comfortable, broken-in walking shoes. Pack at least two pairs. Since you will frequently take your shoes off at dorms and traditional restaurants, pack neat, presentable socks!

## 5. KR Campus Pro-Tips: What to Buy vs. What to Pack
Your suitcase space is valuable. Optimize your luggage limits by relying on local stores for bulky items.

*   **Daiso is Your Best Friend:** Find a multi-story Daiso upon arrival. You can buy bed sheets, hangers, pillows, laundry hampers, slippers, and kitchenware for under 5,000 KRW ($4 USD) per item.
*   **Sizing Considerations:** If you wear shoe sizes larger than EU 44 (US Men's 10.5) or clothing sizes XL and above, pack these from home. Standard Korean retail sizes run smaller and slimmer.
*   **T-Money Card:** Purchase a T-money transit card at any airport convenience store (CU, GS25). It is essential for subways, buses, and taxis.

By packing smart and prioritizing your documents, appropriate electronics, and seasonal basics, you will hit the ground running the moment you land. Welcome to your study abroad adventure in South Korea!
