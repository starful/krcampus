# T-money and Transit in Korea

Navigating a new country can feel daunting, but South Korea boasts one of the most efficient, clean, and affordable public transit systems in the world. As an international student arriving on a D-2 or D-4 visa, mastering the transit system is your ticket to daily freedom. Whether you are commuting to Yonsei University in Seoul or Pusan National University in Busan, you will quickly find that public transport is central to your student life. To get around seamlessly, you will need a smart transit card. In this KR Campus guide, we will break down everything you need to know about T-money, EZL (formerly Cashbee), transit apps, and secret money-saving hacks to keep your student budget intact.

## Understanding Korea's Transit Cards: T-money vs. EZL

The two main prepaid transit cards in Korea are T-money and EZL (formerly known as Cashbee). While both work nationwide on almost all buses, subways, and taxis, they originated from different regions. T-money is the undisputed king of Seoul and the metropolitan area, while EZL has historically been more dominant in Busan and the southern provinces.

Today, you can use either card almost anywhere, but minor differences in partner merchants and regional compatibility remain. As a student, you can purchase these cards at any convenience store (CU, GS25, 7-Eleven, Emart24) or subway ticket booth for about 3,000 to 5,000 KRW.

Let's compare the two primary options:

| Feature | T-money | EZL (Cashbee) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Region** | Seoul & Gyeonggi-do | Busan & Gyeongsang-do |
| **Price** | 3,000 - 5,000 KRW | 3,000 - 5,000 KRW |
| **Nationwide** | Yes | Yes |
| **Buy At** | CVS, Metro stations | CVS, Metro stations |

For most international students, T-money is the default choice due to its massive ecosystem. However, if your university is located in Busan or Daegu, EZL is equally convenient.

## How to Buy, Recharge, and Use Your Transit Card

Once you land at Incheon or Gimhae Airport, your first task should be buying a transit card. You do not need your Residence Card (formerly ARC) to purchase a basic card, meaning you can get one on day one.

**How to Recharge:**
Recharging (called *Chung-jeon* in Korean) is a physical, cash-only process for standard prepaid cards.
*   **Subway Stations:** Head to ticket vending machines, place your card on the reader, select your language, choose the recharge amount, and insert cash.
*   **Convenience Stores:** Hand your card and cash to the cashier and say, "*Chung-jeon hae-ju-se-yo*" (Please recharge this).
*   **Crucial Tip:** Keep cash! You cannot recharge standard prepaid transit cards with credit cards at standard station kiosks.

**Using the Card:**
Simply tap your card against the sensor when boarding a bus or entering a subway turnstile. You will hear a single beep confirming a successful scan, showing your fare and remaining balance.

## Essential Transit Apps for International Students

Navigating South Korea's complex transit routes requires specialized local apps. Because of national security regulations regarding mapping data, Google Maps does not work reliably for walking or transit directions in Korea. You must download local alternatives immediately.

Here are the key apps you need on your phone:
*   **Naver Map:** The absolute gold standard. It offers excellent English support, highly accurate bus arrival times, subway schedules, and precise walking paths.
*   **KakaoMap:** A highly competitive alternative to Naver Map. It features real-time bus tracking (you can see buses moving on the map) and clean user interfaces.
*   **Subway Korea:** A dedicated metro app providing precise schedules, transfer car recommendations, and route maps for all major cities.

| App | Languages | Best Feature | Downside |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Naver Map | EN, KO, ZH, JA | Most accurate | Heavy battery |
| KakaoMap | EN, KO | Real-time tracker | Cluttered UI |
| Subway KR | EN, JA, ZH | Best transfer paths | Subway only |

We highly recommend using Naver Map as your primary navigation tool, supplemented by Subway Korea for complex daily subway commutes.

## Money-Saving Hacks for Seoul and Busan Commutes

Studying abroad can be expensive, but Korea's transit system offers built-in discounts that can save you tens of thousands of Won each month.

*   **The Golden Transfer Rule (Hwan-seung):** Transfers between subways and buses are free if you tap off and board your next transit within 30 minutes (40 mins at night). You can transfer up to 4 times. **Always tap off!** Forgetting to tap off results in a double fare penalty on your next ride.
*   **Seoul Climate Card (Gi-hu-dong-haeng Card):** If you live and study in Seoul, this is a game-changer. For a flat monthly fee of 62,000 KRW (or 65,000 KRW to include the Ttareungi public bicycles), you get unlimited rides on all Seoul subways and buses.
*   **Youth Discounts:** Students aged 13 to 18 get a discounted youth fare. If you are a university student under 24, register your T-money card online (using your ARC number on the official T-money website) to unlock youth-tier discounts on specific commuter passes.
*   **Busan Dongbaek Pass:** If you study in Busan, this local initiative refunds you up to 45,000 KRW in local currency if you spend more than 45,000 KRW on public transit in a single month using their designated card.

## Practical Tips and Transit Etiquette for Students

To ensure a smooth transition into Korean student life, keep these practical tips and cultural etiquettes in mind:
*   **Prepare Cash Early:** Until you get your local Korean bank account and bank-issued transit card (called a Postpaid Check Card), keep small cash bills (1,000, 5,000, and 10,000 KRW) for recharging.
*   **Mind the Priority Seats:** Every subway car has designated seats at the ends for the elderly, pregnant women, and the disabled. Even if the train is packed, locals leave these seats empty.
*   **Keep Your Voice Down:** Talking loudly on the phone or with friends is considered bad etiquette on public transit. Wear headphones and keep conversations quiet.
*   **Get a Postpaid Card Later:** Once your Residence Card (ARC) is issued, open a local bank account and request a Check Card with "Hu-bul Gyotong" (postpaid transit). It bills transit fees directly to your account once a month, eliminating cash recharges!
