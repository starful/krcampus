# Using Clinics and Hospitals in Korea

Welcome to South Korea! As an international student with a D-2 student visa or D-4 language trainee visa, staying healthy is key to your academic success. Fortunately, South Korea boasts one of the world's most advanced and accessible healthcare systems. Navigating medical institutions in a new country might seem daunting, but it is highly streamlined once you understand the basics.

This KR Campus guide covers everything you need to know about using Korean clinics and hospitals, from your National Health Insurance (NHI) copay to practical steps and essential Korean phrases for your visit.

## Understanding Korea's National Health Insurance (NHI)

International students are automatically enrolled in the National Health Insurance (NHI) program once their Alien Registration Card (ARC) is processed. This mandatory insurance grants you the same comprehensive medical benefits as South Korean citizens.

The NHI operates on a copay system. The government covers a large portion of medical fees, while you pay a small out-of-pocket copay. For standard outpatient visits, these costs are remarkably low.

### NHI Copay Rates for Outpatient Care

The percentage you pay depends directly on the type of healthcare facility you visit. 

| Facility Type | Average NHI Coverage | Your Copay Rate | Out-of-Pocket Cost (Routine) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Local Clinic (Uiwon) | 70% – 80% | 20% – 30% | 5,000 – 10,000 KRW |
| General Hospital | 50% – 60% | 40% – 50% | 15,000 – 30,000 KRW |
| University Hospital | 40% | 60% (Needs Referral) | 35,000+ KRW |

*Note: Prescriptions are also heavily subsidized by the NHI, usually costing 3,000 to 6,000 KRW for a standard 3-day supply.*

## Routine Care vs. Emergency Care: Choosing the Right Facility

The South Korean healthcare system is structured into three distinct tiers. Knowing where to go for your symptoms will save you time and money.

| Healthcare Tier | Korean Name | Ideal For | Key Information |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tier 1: Local Clinics** | Uiwon (의원) | Colds, minor injuries, skin issues, checkups | Fast service, no appointment needed. |
| **Tier 2: Hospitals** | Byeongwon (병원) | Minor surgeries, detailed scans (X-ray) | Larger than clinics, slightly longer waits. |
| **Tier 3: General/Uni** | Daehak Byeongwon | Serious illness, major surgery, emergency | Requires a referral slip from Tier 1/2. |

For routine issues like colds, food poisoning, or minor sprains, always visit a local clinic (**Uiwon**) first. These are classified by specialty:
* **Internal Medicine (Naegwa - 내과):** For colds, stomach aches, and general illness.
* **ENT (Ibiinhu-gwa - 이비인후과):** For sore throats, ear infections, and sinus issues.
* **Dermatology (Pibu-gwa - 피부과):** For skin rashes, acne, and allergies.
* **Orthopedics (Jeong-hyeong-oegwa - 정형외과):** For bone, muscle, and joint pain.

If you visit a Tier 3 University Hospital without a referral slip (*Uiroe-seo*) from a local clinic, you will pay high out-of-pocket costs without NHI subsidies.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Your First Clinic Visit

Visiting a local clinic in Korea is fast and efficient. Most clinics operate on a walk-in basis.

1. **Locate a Clinic:** Use Naver Maps or Kakao Maps to search for specialties near your university.
2. **Reception (Jeop-su):** Walk in and present your ARC. If you do not have your physical card yet, present your passport and Certificate of Alien Registration.
3. **Consultation (Jin-ryo):** Meet the doctor. Many doctors in Korea speak conversational or fluent English, especially around major university districts in Seoul.
4. **Payment (Gyeol-je):** Pay your copay at the desk. The receptionist will hand you a receipt and a paper prescription (*Cheo-bang-jeon*).
5. **The Pharmacy (Yak-guk):** Take your prescription to any nearby pharmacy (marked with a bright red **약** sign). The pharmacist will prepare your medicine and explain the dosage.

Korean pharmacies pack pills into individual paper packets labeled for breakfast (*Achim*), lunch (*Jeom-sim*), and dinner (*Jeo-nyeok*).

## Handling Emergencies and After-Hours Care

If you experience an emergency at night or during the weekend when clinics are closed, follow these protocols:

* **Call 119 for an Ambulance:** This service is free. Say "English please" to connect with an interpreter.
* **Emergency Rooms (Eung-geup-sil):** You can walk into any university hospital ER. Note that ER visits for non-emergencies incur heavy surcharges not covered by the NHI.
* **Late-Night Pharmacies (Sim-ya Yak-guk):** While standard pharmacies close around 8:00 PM, some designated areas operate late-night pharmacies.
* **Convenience Stores:** For minor pain relief (Tylenol), digestive pills, or medical patches, visit any convenience store (GS25, CU). They sell basic over-the-counter medicines (*Sang-bi-yak*) 24/7.

## Essential Korean Phrases for Your Medical Visit

While many doctors speak English, receptionist and pharmacy staff may not. These phrases will help you communicate easily:

### At the Clinic Reception Desk
* "Hello, I would like to see a doctor."
  * *An-nyeong-ha-se-yo. Jin-ryo bat-eu-reo wa-sseo-yo.* (안녕하세요. 진료 받으러 왔어요.)
* "Here is my registration card."
  * *We-guk-in deung-rok-jeung yeo-gi i-sseo-yo.* (외국인 등록증 여기 있어요.)
* "Is there an English-speaking doctor?"
  * *Yeong-eo ha-neun ui-sa seon-saeng-nim gye-sin-ga-yo?* (영어 하는 의사 선생님 계신가요?)

### Describing Your Symptoms
* "I have a cold."
  * *Gam-gi-e geol-lyeo-sseo-yo.* (감기에 걸렸어요.)
* "My stomach hurts."
  * *Bae-ga a-pa-yo.* (배가 아파요.)
* "I have a fever and headache."
  * *Yeol-i na-go du-tong-i i-sseo-yo.* (열이 나고 두통이 있어요.)
* "I hurt my ankle."
  * *Bal-mok-eul da-chyeo-sseo-yo.* (발목을 다쳤어요.)

### At the Pharmacy
* "Please give me this medicine."
  * *I yak ju-se-yo.* (이 약 주세요.)
* "How many times a day do I take this?"
  * *Ha-ru-e myeot beon meog-eo-ya hae-yo?* (하루에 몇 번 먹어야 해요?)

## Staying Healthy as an International Student

Taking care of your health is a vital part of your study abroad experience. Thanks to Korea's comprehensive NHI system, you can receive top-tier medical care without financial stress. Keep your ARC with you, visit a local clinic when you feel unwell, and make use of campus health centers for basic issues. Stay safe, healthy, and enjoy your academic journey in South Korea!
